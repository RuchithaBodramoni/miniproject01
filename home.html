The Bhagavad Gita

About

The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of God") is a sacred scripture in Hindu philosophy, forming part of the Indian epic Mahabharata. Comprising 700 verses, it is a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna, who serves as his charioteer. The conversation occurs on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, where Arjuna is overcome with moral confusion and despair about fighting in a war against his own relatives and teachers.
Krishna imparts spiritual wisdom and guidance, addressing fundamental questions about duty (dharma), righteousness, and the nature of life, the self (atman), and the universe. The Gita discusses paths to spiritual realization, including devotion (bhakti), knowledge (jnana), and disciplined action (karma). It has had a profound influence on spiritual thought worldwide, serving as a guide for ethical living and self-realization.

Arjuna Vishada Yoga

"Arjuna Vishada Yoga" is the first chapter of the Bhagavad Gita, also known as the Yoga of Arjuna's Dejection. The first chapter of the Bhagavad Gita, portrays Arjuna's moral dilemma and emotional turmoil on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. As he prepares to fight, Arjuna is overcome with sorrow and confusion at the prospect of battling his own relatives, teachers, and friends. He questions the righteousness of war, fearing it will lead to destruction, societal chaos, and spiritual downfall. Unable to resolve his inner conflict, he surrenders to Lord Krishna and seeks guidance. This chapter sets the stage for the spiritual teachings of the Gita, highlighting the themes of duty, morality, and the search for wisdom in moments of despair.
